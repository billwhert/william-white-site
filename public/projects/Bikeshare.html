Structure Analysis Review:¶

1) NY has 54,770 rows with 9 columns. Start and End times are currently "Factor" typewhich need to be converted to datetime. There also appear to be "" values in multiple columns to be aware of if those columns are necessary for analysis and answering the questions. These will be treated as missing values and not a separate category.

2) Ny has a stray value of "201" under End.Time which could make parsing to datetime prone to error. End.Time is not needed for the related questions and will be removed from the working dataset, along with a few other columns, prior to analysis and visualization.

3) Wash has 89,051 rows but only 7 columns (missing Gender and Birth Year). These will also be dropped in the final merged dataset and are overall not necessary for the three questions being asked, but were pointed out for clarity.

4) Trip duration for NY and Chi are int, but Wash duration is num. These will need to be a a consistent num type for precision and better percentile/quantile statistical functions.

5) Chi only 8,630 rows but similar issues to NY.

Summary Analysis Review¶

1) Max duration on all 3 cities show some extreme outlier values (1088634 seconds is over 302 hours). This would be a bikeshare trip for over 12.5 days which is unrealistic. Extreme values will be capped as there should be little effect on the mean and the values appear to be clear errors when compared to the other quantiles and averages.

2) NA's are identified for NY and Wash for 1 trip duration in each city. These will be imputed to the median to provide complete, logical values. Because there are going to be thousands and thousands of trips on the lower-end, the median value should be safer in this particular project in case of a few relatively high values pulling the average up.

3) There are also illogical birth years listed (e.g. 1885 and 1899, making people over 100 years old when this data would have been recorded). As with previous items, birth year data is unnecessary to answer or analyze the questions presented. These fields will be dropped when creating the working tables.

Cleaning Steps Before Merging¶

For each city, I trimmed each down to a table containing Start.Time, Trip.Duration, User.Type, and a new City field.

I converted Start.Time from Factor to POSIXct and retrieved the hour/minute values for time-of-day analysis.

I standardized Trip.Duration to numeric (seconds) and applied a 99th percentile cap to deal with extreme outliers.

I cleaned User.Type by trimming whitespace and converting blanks (e.g. "") to “Unknown” so these bikeshares are retained but not confused as real categories. After capping, I imputed a small number of missing durations by using the field's median value.

Finally, I aligned column names and classes across cities to ensure row merging is successful for cross-city comparisons while dropping unused columns (e.g., End.Time, Gender, Birth.Year) from the working tables but leaving them in the raw data.

Analysis and testing¶

Question 1¶

Does the bikeshare service have more customers or subscribers using the service?

$H_0$: Among rides, the proportion of Subscribers equals the proportion of Customers both overall and in each city.

$H_1$: The proportions are not equal and Subscribers have a greater proportion than Customers.

Question 3 Summary
Again, we must reject the null hypothesis for question 3 as the average ride length is different for each city. Average ride for Washington DC is approximately 19 minutes, Chicago is approximately 15 minutes, and New York City is approximately 13 min.

Pairwise Wilcoxon testing was used to confirm that all city pairs differ (p-value < 0.001) while being robust to skews. The ANOVA test, using log durations to adjust for the tail of long ride durations (logical, as the bikes are meant for short rentals to nearby locations), also helps to verify that we reject the null hypothesis (i.e., p-value < .001). The chances that the city averages being so far apart and based on "luck" are effectively 0.

DC, by far, contributed the most hours overall for rideshares even adjusting for the skew on total riders.
